# How to Read the Data

*Student glossary and decision guide*

Use this with the app and your packet so the evidence stays clear, fair, and useful.

---

## How this inquiry works

1. First ask what looks strong on opportunity.
2. Then ask whether it still feels reachable for Hickman Mills students.
3. Then ask where someone could realistically start.
4. Finally, connect the evidence back to your own plan and to PREP-KC's next-step decisions.

---

## Key terms in plain language

| Term | What it means in this project | Question to ask yourself |
|------|------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Opportunity** | A pathway looks promising because it has meaningful demand, enough scale to matter, and wages or advancement that make it worth considering. | Is this pathway strong enough to matter before I think about my personal preferences? |
| **Current scale** | How big the pathway already is in the region right now. | Is this a niche path or a pathway with enough real jobs to matter? |
| **Projected demand / openings signal** | A sign that jobs in this pathway are likely to keep showing up over time. | Does this pathway seem durable, or is it only big right now? |
| **Median wage** | A middle-point wage signal. It does not tell you everything, but it helps compare earning potential. | Is the wage strong enough to make the pathway feel worth it? |
| **County fit** | Which counties seem strongest for this pathway and where the opportunity is concentrated. | Would this answer change if I think about where students actually live and travel? |
| **Bridge role** | A believable first job or early step that helps someone move toward a bigger goal. | Can I picture a realistic first role, not just the end career? |
| **Reachability** | Whether students can realistically get to this option from where they are now. | Does this path still feel real when I think about commute, time, cost, and access? |
| **Supports** | What students would need in order to benefit from a pathway: transportation, advising, paid work-based learning, confidence, information, and more. | What would make this pathway actually usable, not just attractive on paper? |
| **Launch point** | A realistic first place to start: a program, college, training route, or apprenticeship entry point. | Where could someone really begin? |
| **Transfer anchor** | A place that helps students continue into the next step after a first credential or early coursework. | Does this option help students keep moving, not just start? |
| **Pell share / accessibility** | A clue about which institutions tend to serve students with greater financial need and broad access. | Does this place seem reachable, or only impressive? |
| **Linked occupations** | How many occupations or job types connect to a program or pathway in the dataset. | Does this path connect to more than one possible job outcome? |

## What the data can — and cannot — tell you

| Helpful use of the data | Unhelpful use of the data |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Compare pathways using more than one kind of evidence. | Choose a pathway only because one number is the biggest. |
| Notice when a pathway looks strong overall but harder to reach from Hickman Mills. | Assume that "strong in Kansas City" automatically means "reachable for us." |
| Use launch points to identify realistic starts. | Skip straight to prestige or destination schools. |
| Treat wages as one signal among several. | Assume the highest-paying path is automatically the best path. |
| Use missing or small-sample data as a reason to ask careful questions. | Act like missing data means a pathway has no value. |
| Let the evidence sharpen your thinking about your own future. | Treat the data as a final judgment on your life plan. |

## Questions worth asking on every screen

### Socratic reminders

- What looks strong here — and what evidence makes me say that?
- What changes once I think about Hickman Mills students specifically?
- What would make this pathway feel real, not just impressive?
- What first step can I actually picture?
- What would I still need to know before making a recommendation?

### When a data label sounds technical

- Pause and translate it into plain English before you use it in an argument.
- If you cannot explain a field clearly, do not build your case around it yet.
- Ask your teacher or note the question in your evidence catcher so the class can clarify it.
